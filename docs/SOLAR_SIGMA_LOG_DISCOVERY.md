Large events occur **throughout** bursts, indicating complex cascading where flares trigger more flares.

### 6.4 Energy Correlation

**Consecutive flare energy correlation: r = 0.387 (p < 0.0001)**

Big flares **beget** big flares. This is sympathetic/triggered behavior, not independent events.

### 6.5 Physical Interpretation

The CASCADE MODEL:
1. **TRIGGERING:** Flares destabilize nearby magnetic structures
2. **SYMPATHETIC FLARING:** Energy release triggers magnetically-connected regions
3. **AVALANCHE BEHAVIOR:** Self-organized criticality — small perturbations trigger large releases

**Why not simple charging?**
If flares were purely "charge then discharge":
- Longer wait = more energy stored = bigger flare

The NEGATIVE correlation proves flares are TRIGGERED, not spontaneous.

1. Compute log-flux: ln(F_i)
2. Fit normal distribution to log-flux
3. σ_log = standard deviation of fit

## 12. Limitations and Open Questions

### 12.1 Sample Size at Minimum

Solar minimum years (2017-2020) have few flares (N < 100), limiting statistical precision.

**Mitigation:** The convergence to σ_log ≈ 1.5 appears in both 2017 (N=67) and 2021 (N=101) independently.

### 12.2 Detection Threshold Effects

GOES detects flares above ~C1.0 reliably. Weaker flares may be missed, potentially biasing the distribution.

**Impact:** Would truncate the low-energy tail, potentially **increasing** apparent σ_log. The solar minimum values might be even closer to 1.5 with complete detection.

### 12.3 Flare Definition

DONKI catalogs "events" which may include multiple peaks or extended episodes. Definition inconsistencies could affect statistics.

**Mitigation:** Use consistent source (DONKI) throughout; trends should be robust to definition.

### 12.4 Causation vs. Correlation

We observe σ_log varying with solar cycle. We **hypothesize** this reflects impedance dynamics. Definitive proof requires:
- Theoretical derivation of σ_log from MHD
- Independent measurement of magnetic impedance
- Controlled experiments (impossible for the Sun)
